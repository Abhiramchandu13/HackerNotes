# IEEE 802.11

> IEEE 802.11 is the universal set of engineering standards that define exactly how Wi-Fi technology must operate at the physical and data link layers.

## Overview
When you buy a Dell laptop, an Apple iPhone, and a Netgear router, they all connect flawlessly over Wi-Fi. This interoperability exists solely because all manufacturers adhere to the **IEEE 802.11** family of specifications. 

The Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers (IEEE) created the 802.11 working group to define the exact radio frequencies, modulation techniques, frame formats, and security handshakes that make wireless networking possible.

## Why It Matters
When reading vendor documentation, researching wireless security, or configuring an enterprise wireless controller, you must understand 802.11 terminology. "Wi-Fi" is just a consumer marketing term; "802.11ac" is the actual engineering reality. Understanding the different standard amendments (the letters after 802.11) tells an engineer exactly what speeds, ranges, and security protocols a device supports.

## Core Concepts
- **The Standard:** 802.11 sits alongside 802.3 (Ethernet) and 802.15 (Bluetooth).
- **Amendments (The Letters):** As technology improves, the IEEE publishes amendments. `802.11b` was early Wi-Fi, `802.11ax` is modern Wi-Fi.
- **Backward Compatibility:** A core feature of the standards. A modern `802.11ax` router will still happily talk to an ancient `802.11g` laptop, though it must slow down its radio specifically to communicate with that device.
- **Wi-Fi Alliance Branding:** Because "802.11ax" confuses consumers, the Wi-Fi Alliance marketing group rebranded the generations to simpler numbers (e.g., "Wi-Fi 6").

## Components / Types (The Evolution)
Here are the major standards you must know:
- **802.11b (Wi-Fi 1 - 1999):** 2.4 GHz only. Max 11 Mbps. Obsolete.
- **802.11g (Wi-Fi 3 - 2003):** 2.4 GHz only. Max 54 Mbps. Obsolete.
- **802.11n (Wi-Fi 4 - 2009):** Uses both 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz. Max 600 Mbps. Introduced **MIMO** (Multiple Input, Multiple Output antennas). Still very common in IoT devices.
- **802.11ac (Wi-Fi 5 - 2013):** 5 GHz only. Max 1+ Gbps. Massively widened channel bandwidths. The current baseline for corporate networks.
- **802.11ax (Wi-Fi 6 / 6E - 2019):** Uses 2.4, 5, and 6 GHz. Designed not for raw speed, but for **High Density**. It introduces OFDMA, allowing the router to talk to multiple different devices at the exact same millisecond, virtually eliminating latency in crowded stadiums or offices.

## How It Works
The standard dictates the shape of the data. An 802.11 frame looks very different from an 802.3 Ethernet frame.
1. A laptop prepares an 802.11 frame. 
2. Unlike Ethernet (which has 2 MAC addresses: Source and Destination), an 802.11 frame actually has **four** MAC address fields. It must track the Source, Destination, Transmitter (the Access Point), and Receiver.
3. The standard dictates the complex CSMA/CA backoff timers the laptop must use to avoid colliding with other radios before transmitting the frame.

## Practical Examples
- **Enterprise Purchasing:** An IT director is buying 500 new Access Points. They specifically request "802.11ax Wave 2" access points to ensure the hardware supports the latest high-density OFDMA features required for a crowded university lecture hall.

## Security Considerations
- **Standard Flaws (WEP):** Early 802.11 standards included a security protocol called WEP (Wired Equivalent Privacy). WEP's cryptographic math was so deeply flawed within the IEEE standard that it can be cracked in under 3 minutes today.
- **802.11w (PMF):** Protected Management Frames. A newer amendment to the standard that encrypts the control traffic (like Deauthentication frames). Without PMF, attackers can easily kick users off a network. With PMF enabled, Deauth attacks fail.
- **802.11i (WPA2):** The amendment that introduced robust AES encryption, rescuing Wi-Fi from the disaster of WEP.

## Commands / Configuration Examples
### Linux (Identifying Device Capabilities)
```bash
# Display the detailed 802.11 capabilities of your wireless card
iw list

# You will see output listing supported bands, frequencies, and standards
# Example: "Band 1: Frequencies: 2412 MHz [1] (20.0 dBm)"
```

### Cisco WLC (Disabling Legacy Rates)
To keep a network fast, admins often explicitly disable support for legacy 802.11b rates.
```text
! Disabling the slow 1, 2, 5.5, and 11 Mbps data rates ensures old 
! devices don't drag down the speed of the entire network.
config 802.11b rate disabled 1
config 802.11b rate disabled 2
config 802.11b rate disabled 5.5
config 802.11b rate disabled 11
```

## Troubleshooting
- **Legacy Device Drag:** If an old 802.11g printer connects to your modern 802.11ac network, the Access Point must physically shift its radio down to the slow speeds to talk to the printer. Because Wi-Fi is a shared medium, this consumes massive amounts of "airtime," drastically slowing down the internet for the modern laptops nearby.
- **Driver Incompatibility:** Sometimes an older laptop with an outdated network driver will outright refuse to connect to a new 802.11ax router, or the router will crash the laptop's driver.

## Interview Questions
- Which IEEE standard governs wireless networking?
- Match "Wi-Fi 6" to its official IEEE 802.11 amendment name. (Answer: 802.11ax).
- What was the primary focus of the 802.11ax standard compared to older standards? (Answer: High-density performance and efficiency using OFDMA, rather than just raw speed).
- Why do network engineers often disable 802.11b data rates on enterprise access points?

## Summary
IEEE 802.11 is the architectural blueprint of Wi-Fi. By progressing through alphabetical amendments (b, g, n, ac, ax), the standard has steadily evolved wireless networking from a slow, unreliable novelty into a multi-gigabit technology capable of supporting thousands of dense, simultaneous connections.
